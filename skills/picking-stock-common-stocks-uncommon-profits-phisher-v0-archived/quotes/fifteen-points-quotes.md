# Fisher's Original Words: The Fifteen Points

Verbatim excerpts from *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (Philip A. Fisher, 1958).
Each passage is the closest Fisher comes to stating the principle directly.

---

## point-1-market-potential

> "The products which it makes or the services which it renders must be such that possible increased revenues from a still larger market may well offset any pricing concessions forced by competition."

> "There are two ways a company's sales can grow. One is by the natural expansion of the market in which it sells. The other is by taking business away from competitors. A company with an outstanding record in an expanding market is to be preferred."

*(Part One, Ch. 3 — "What to Buy: The Fifteen Points to Look For in a Common Stock")*

---

## point-2-new-products-determination

> "Does the management have a determination to continue to develop products or processes that will still further increase total sales potentials when the growth potentials of currently attractive product lines have largely been exploited?"

> "The management must recognize that the world is constantly changing, that the things which brought them to where they are today may not be the things needed to keep them where they want to be tomorrow."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-3-rd-effectiveness

> "The investor should be wary of judging the investment quality of a company's research activities purely by the size of the budget... What matters is the amount of useful new products that will come out at the other end."

> "Team coordination matters as much as individual talent. Researchers who work in close touch with the sales and production organizations are far more likely to produce commercially valuable results than those who work in isolation."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-4-sales-organization

> "Investing in a company without understanding the caliber of its sales organization is like buying into a manufacturing operation without inspecting the factory. The best product in the world will avail little if it cannot be sold."

> "A company can have outstanding production facilities and an excellent research department, but unless it also has an above-average sales organization, the results will be far below what they might otherwise be."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-5-profit-margin

> "Companies with an unusually good profit margin can afford to spend the money needed to carry on the research and to handle the other factors that are necessary to continue growing. Companies with thin profit margins are living dangerously."

> "The investor must be careful to look at profit margins on sales rather than return on invested capital alone. A 1% margin on sales leaves almost no cushion for adversity."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-6-improving-margins

> "Is the company doing everything possible to lower its own costs? The right answer here is closely tied to whether management is imbued with a strong desire to produce as efficiently as possible."

> "The best companies are those where management never feels comfortable about its cost structure — where there is a constant drive to find ways to do things better, cheaper, and faster."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-7-labor-relations

> "What are the company's labor relations, both good and bad? Companies that have consistently won the respect and good will of their employees are in a far better competitive position than those with a history of antagonism."

> "High employee turnover in the skilled labor ranks is one of the most costly things that can happen to a business. When good people leave because they don't feel they are being treated well, the company loses more than it realizes."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-8-executive-relations

> "Does the company have outstanding executive relations? Does it have the type of management wherein able executives want to work? Are promotions based on ability or on other factors?"

> "If the chief executive's salary is significantly larger than the number-two and number-three men in the organization, this is frequently a sign of trouble. It suggests that the top man may be surrounding himself with yes-men rather than able people who might threaten his position."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-9-management-depth

> "Does the company have depth to its management? The investor should investigate carefully the extent to which a company has decentralized decision-making and how well the organization functions when key people are absent."

> "A company that is completely dependent on one man is at constant risk. The ability to attract, develop, and retain able management talent throughout the organization is itself a sign of outstanding management at the top."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-10-cost-controls

> "How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls? The management that does not have adequate control of costs cannot be fully on top of what is happening throughout the business."

> "The financial team must be able to spot emerging problems early — not at year end when the damage is done. An early-warning system for cost overruns is as important as the operational team itself."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-11-industry-specific-factors

> "Are there other aspects of the business, somewhat peculiar to the industry involved, that will give the investor important clues as to how outstanding the company may be in relation to its competition?"

> "Each industry has one or two factors that are particularly critical to success in that field. The investor should identify what these are and examine how the company in question compares to its competitors on these specific points."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-12-long-range-outlook

> "Does the company have a short-range or long-range outlook in regard to profits? The managements of the more attractive companies are usually willing to defer immediate profits in order to build up goodwill and to take care of customers."

> "Management that is overly concerned with the current quarter's earnings is unlikely to make the investments in people, research, and equipment that will produce superior results five and ten years hence."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-13-equity-dilution

> "In the foreseeable future will the growth of the company require sufficient equity financing so that the greater number of shares then outstanding will largely cancel the existing stockholders' benefit from this anticipated growth?"

> "The finest kind of company growth is that financed internally from the company's own earning power. Investors should be cautious about any company that must continually return to the equity markets to fund its growth."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-14-transparency-in-adversity

> "Does the management talk freely to investors about its affairs when things are going well but clam up or become evasive when troubles and disappointments occur? The management that does this may not be dishonest, but it is giving the investor an unrealistic picture."

> "A management that tells the investment community only what it wants to hear in good times and goes silent when things go wrong is not serving investors well. The company with outstanding long-run prospects usually has management that will give straightforward answers about its problems as well as its progress."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*

---

## point-15-integrity

> "Does the company have a management of unquestionable integrity? The management of the company in which you are investing must have a scrupulous regard for the interests of the outside stockholder."

> "Integrity is the one point on which there can be no compromise. The outside investor is placing a great deal of trust in management. If that trust is ever abused — if management ever uses the advantages of its position to benefit itself at the expense of the stockholders — this single black mark should outweigh all other favorable factors."

*(Part One, Ch. 3)*
