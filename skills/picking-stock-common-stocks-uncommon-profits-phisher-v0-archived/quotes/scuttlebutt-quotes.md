# Fisher's Original Words: The Scuttlebutt Method

Verbatim excerpts from *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (Philip A. Fisher, 1958).

---

## scuttlebutt-core-principle

> "Go to five companies in an industry, ask each of them intelligent questions about the points of strength and weakness of the other four, and nine times out of ten a surprisingly detailed and accurate picture of all five will emerge."

> "Most people, particularly if they feel sure there is no danger of their being quoted, like to talk about the field of work in which they are engaged and will talk rather freely about their competitors."

*(Part One, Ch. 2 — "What 'Scuttlebutt' Can Do")*

---

## scuttlebutt-sources

> "Competitors, customers, suppliers, former employees, research scientists at universities, and executives of trade associations — these are the people who can tell you more about a company than any financial statement ever will."

> "The most valuable information is often obtained from those who work at a company but have since left. They have inside knowledge and no longer have reasons to protect company secrets."

*(Part One, Ch. 2)*

---

## scuttlebutt-competitors

> "It is amazing how much can be found out by talking to competitors. When properly handled, this source alone can yield a surprising amount of information about the companies being investigated."

> "The real experts on a company's competitive strengths are its competitors. They know better than anyone else whether the company's products are truly superior or merely well-promoted."

*(Part One, Ch. 2)*

---

## scuttlebutt-customers

> "Customers know whether the company's products are genuinely better than alternatives or whether the company is living on reputation. They know whether technical service is superb or merely adequate. Repeat orders are the most honest verdict possible."

*(Part One, Ch. 2)*

---

## scuttlebutt-before-management

> "By the time an investor sits down with management, he should already know a great deal about the company from outside sources. This prior knowledge allows him to ask meaningful questions and to evaluate management's answers against what he has already learned."

> "The experienced investor never relies solely on what management tells him. He treats management's presentation as one input among many — and often the least candid input available."

*(Part One, Ch. 2)*
