# Skill: Repeatable Supergroups (Creating Superfans - Module 7)

**Knowledge source:** *Creating Superfans* by Brittany Hodak.
**Reference library:** `references/case_library.md`
**Standalone use:** This module can run independently for scaling customer experience or as the final systematization step after other modules.

## When to Use

Use this module when the user asks how to scale customer experience, make great service repeatable, align teams around customers, improve employee enablement, apply SUPER to marketing, sales, social media, support, or leadership, or create a feedback loop.

## Module Inputs

- Existing superfan-creating behaviors
- Team ownership and handoffs
- Current checklists, CRM triggers, automations, and training
- Employee experience constraints
- Customer feedback sources and review rhythms
- Metrics for retention, referrals, reviews, support, and advocacy

## Module Outputs

Return a repeatable operating system with:
- Behaviors to eliminate, automate, delegate, defer, or document
- Team responsibilities
- Checklist or CRM trigger
- Employee enablement need
- Measurement and review cadence

## Execution Flow

### Overview

```text
Identify best behavior -> remove founder dependency -> document and train -> align teams -> create feedback and measurement loop
```

### Step 1: Turn excellent behavior into a system

**Core question:** Which superfan-creating behavior currently depends on one person?

**Decision rules:**

| Signal | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Founder or one employee creates most memorable moments | Document and train |
| Manual task adds little customer value | Eliminate, automate, delegate, or defer |
| Great service varies by employee | Checklist and standards needed |
| Feedback is collected but not acted on | Create routing and review rhythm |

**Ask the user:** Which excellent customer behavior would disappear if one person left?

**Citation instruction:** When emphasizing repeatability, cite `#one-repeatable-action`, `#harbaugh-consistency`, or `#measuring-experience`.

### Step 2: Include employee experience

**Core question:** Are employees supported enough to create superfans?

**Decision rules:**

| Strong system | Weak system |
|---------------|-------------|
| Employees know the story, standards, and authority | Employees are expected to improvise without support |
| Frontline feedback changes operations | Feedback dies in dashboards |
| Leaders remove friction for customer-facing teams | Leaders demand care while creating employee apathy |

**Ask the user:** What prevents employees from delivering the experience the brand promises?

**Citation instruction:** When connecting internal and external experience, cite the closest repeatability or customer-centricity anchor in `references/case_library.md`.

### Step 3: Apply SUPER across supergroups

**Core question:** Which team can create or destroy the customer experience?

**Decision rules:**

| Team | SUPER application |
|------|-------------------|
| Marketing | Reinforce story and transformation, not just features |
| Sales | Listen first, sell transformation, follow through |
| Service | Treat support as reputation-shaping and recovery-capable |
| Social media | Build relationships, listen, respond, and solve |
| Leadership | Route voice-of-customer into visible changes |

**Ask the user:** Which handoff or department most often breaks the customer promise?

**Citation instruction:** When explaining organization-wide customer centricity, cite `#every-interaction-matters`, `#measuring-experience`, or `#create-experiences-worth-talking-about`.

## Do Not

- Do not let customer experience depend on heroics.
- Do not measure only speed or completion while ignoring care, clarity, and memory.
- Do not demand customer enthusiasm while ignoring employee apathy.
- Do not collect feedback without routing it into operational changes.
