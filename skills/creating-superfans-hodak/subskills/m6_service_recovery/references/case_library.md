# Creating Superfans — Apology and Recovery Extracts

Verbatim excerpts from *Creating Superfans: Turning Everyday Customers into Enthusiastic Advocates* (Brittany Hodak, 2023).

---

## the-five-as-of-apology

> "There are five steps to a proper apology—the 5 As. Together, they constitute the correct way to make amends."

> "1. Acknowledge — For an apology to feel authentic, you must begin by acknowledging what went wrong."

> "2. Apologize — Say 'I'm sorry,' and mean it."

> "3. Ask — 'How can I make this right?'"

> "4. Atone — This is where you put the solution into action."

> "5. Adjust — Once you've resolved your customer's problem, the final part of each apology should be to adjust whatever system, process, or behavior caused the issue."

## apologize-without-excuses

> "One of my favorite Benjamin Franklin quotes is 'Never ruin an apology with an excuse.' It was good advice when he said it, and it's held up over time. Customers don't want excuses—at least not as part of the apology. 'I'm sorry' is a complete sentence. No qualifiers, and no excuses."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## company-cant-be-sorry

> "It's also worth noting that a company can't be sorry because a company is not a person (regardless of what the Supreme Court says). Companies do not have emotions or feelings. So, if you find yourself writing an apology email or note, never sign it with just the company name."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## ask-how-to-make-it-right

> "Ask, 'How can I make this right?' and then give your customer the opportunity to tell you. They might propose something along the lines of what you have in mind, or an entirely different idea. Regardless, it's important to give the person you've wronged in some way the opportunity to suggest what you can do to make it right."

> "By inviting them into the process, you increase the likelihood that they will be satisfied with the outcome."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## dont-compare-customers

> "Another no-no is comparing any customer's experience to that of another. Just don't—ever!"

> "Your customer doesn't care about anyone else's experience. If anything, comparisons make situations worse, not better."

## customer-not-always-right

> "The adage that 'the customer is always right' is not only incorrect and outdated, it's also potentially harmful in its widespread acceptance. The customer isn't always right. And, sometimes, a customer simply isn't right for your business."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## protect-employees-from-bad-customers

> "Don't let bad customers cost you good employees. While customers are entitled to complain or to voice legitimate concerns, if those complaints turn combative, it's a problem."

> "Employees can only support customers if they are first supported by the rest of their team. If you allow bad customers to be abusive, your employees can feel unappreciated and will be more likely to provide inferior service to all customers, good and bad alike."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## bad-customers-steal-resources

> "In addition to demoralizing employees, bad customers also steal resources away from good customers. Dealing with bad apples means expending efforts that could be otherwise directed to serving good customers."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*

## fire-bad-customers

> "Bad customers will always cost you more than they're worth."

> "Don't be afraid to 'fire' bad customers."

> "Once you've decided a customer or client relationship is over, it needs to be over. No trial separations, no 'let's give it some time and see how we feel' nonsense, and no back-pedaling. Rip off the Band-Aid."

*(Chapter 13: Back 2 Good)*
