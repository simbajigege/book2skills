# Skill: Service Recovery (Creating Superfans - Module 6)

**Knowledge source:** *Creating Superfans* by Brittany Hodak.
**Reference library:** `references/case_library.md`
**Standalone use:** This module can run independently when there is a customer failure, complaint, bad review, refund issue, or support escalation.

## When to Use

Use this module when the user asks what to do after a bad customer experience, how to apologize, how to respond to an angry customer, how to recover trust, whether to compensate a customer, or whether to set boundaries with an abusive or wrong-fit customer.

## Module Inputs

- What happened, from the customer's point of view
- Customer impact and emotional state
- Company's current response, if any
- Relationship value and history
- Whether the customer behavior is abusive, fraudulent, or wrong-fit
- What process allowed the failure

## Module Outputs

Return a service recovery plan with:
- Failure summary from the customer's view
- 5 As plan: acknowledge, apologize, ask, atone, adjust
- Draft message or script when useful
- Repair, boundary, or exit recommendation
- Prevention change

## Execution Flow

### Overview

```text
Name the failure -> apply 5 As -> decide repair/boundary/exit -> draft response -> fix the system
```

### Step 1: Summarize the failure from the customer's perspective

**Core question:** What did the customer experience, not what did the company intend?

**Decision rules:**

| Strong recovery start | Weak recovery start |
|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Names the failure and its impact | Minimizes, deflects, or explains first |
| Uses a real human voice | Hides behind "the company" |
| Avoids comparisons to other customers | Tells the customer others were fine |

**Ask the user:** What exact harm, inconvenience, embarrassment, cost, or disappointment did the customer experience?

**Citation instruction:** When starting recovery, cite `#acknowledge-without-excuses`.

### Step 2: Apply the 5 As

**Core question:** Has the company repaired both the emotion and the system?

**Decision rules:**

| A | Required action |
|---|-----------------|
| Acknowledge | Name the failure and impact |
| Apologize | Say sorry without qualifiers or excuses |
| Ask | Invite the customer to say what would make it right |
| Atone | Execute a meaningful fix |
| Adjust | Change the process to reduce recurrence |

**Ask the user:** What would make this right from the customer's perspective, and what must change internally?

**Citation instruction:** When using the framework, cite `#the-five-as-of-apology`, `#apologize-without-excuses`, `#ask-how-to-make-it-right`, or `#adjust-prevent-recurrence`.

### Step 3: Decide repair, boundary, or exit

**Core question:** Is this a good customer to recover, a relationship that needs boundaries, or a wrong-fit customer to end?

**Decision rules:**

| Customer behavior | Recommendation |
|-------------------|----------------|
| Reasonable customer harmed by company failure | Repair aggressively |
| Upset but respectful customer | Repair and communicate prevention |
| Abusive or exploitative customer | Protect employees and set boundaries |
| Wrong-fit customer draining resources from better customers | Consider ending relationship |

**Ask the user:** Did the customer cross a line that harms employees or other customers?

**Citation instruction:** For boundary cases, cite `#customer-not-always-right`, `#protect-employees-from-bad-customers`, or `#fire-bad-customers`.

## Do Not

- Do not write an excuse-filled apology.
- Do not compare the customer to other customers.
- Do not compensate without fixing the root cause.
- Do not let abusive customers harm employees or better customers.
