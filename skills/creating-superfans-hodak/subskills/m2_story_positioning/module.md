# Skill: Story Positioning (Creating Superfans - Module 2)

**Knowledge source:** *Creating Superfans* by Brittany Hodak.
**Reference library:** `references/case_library.md`
**Standalone use:** This module can run independently or after M1 diagnosis.

## When to Use

Use this module when the user asks how to differentiate, clarify the brand story, create a category of one, improve messaging, or make customers care beyond features and price.

## Module Inputs

- Brand origin or founder story
- Product or service category
- Target customer's desired identity or transformation
- Current positioning, tagline, pitch, or sales message
- Competitors and points of parity

## Module Outputs

Return a story positioning report with:
- Brand story raw material
- Customer story intersection
- Category-of-one point
- Messaging changes for marketing, sales, onboarding, or service

## Execution Flow

### Overview

```text
Extract origin -> name customer-relevant truth -> find story intersection -> define category-of-one point -> convert to touchpoint language
```

### Step 1: Extract the real story

**Core question:** Why does this organization exist beyond selling the product?

**Decision rules:**

| Strong story | Weak story |
|--------------|------------|
| Explains origin, values, purpose, and customer relevance | Lists features, credentials, slogans, or generic mission claims |
| Can be retold by employees in their own words | Depends on polished marketing language |
| Gives customers a reason to identify with the brand | Only tells customers what the company sells |

**Ask the user:** What happened that made this business need to exist? What belief or standard would you refuse to compromise?

**Citation instruction:** When explaining why story matters, cite `#story-core-principle` or `#origin-story-purpose`.

### Step 2: Find the story intersection

**Core question:** Where does the brand's story intersect with the customer's story?

**Decision rules:**

| Signal | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| Customer sees their desired identity in the brand | Strong story intersection |
| Customer sees only features or price | Weak story intersection |
| Story speaks to a transformation customers want | Differentiation can become emotional, not just functional |

**Ask the user:** What does choosing this brand help the customer say about themselves?

**Citation instruction:** When naming the intersection, cite `#superfans-created-at-intersection` or `#story-identity-connection`.

### Step 3: Define the category-of-one point

**Core question:** What makes this brand meaningfully different in a way customers can feel?

**Decision rules:**

| Strong category-of-one point | Weak point |
|------------------------------|------------|
| Specific, believable, and customer-relevant | "Better service", "high quality", or "we care" |
| Visible in multiple touchpoints | Only appears in a tagline |
| Hard for competitors to copy without changing behavior | Easy to copy in copywriting |

**Ask the user:** Which behavior proves the story before the customer has to trust your words?

**Citation instruction:** When discussing category creation, cite `#category-of-one`.

## Output Format

```text

Brand story:
- [origin / belief / promise]

Customer story intersection:
- [customer identity, struggle, or transformation]

Category-of-one point:
- [specific differentiator]

Touchpoint translation:
- Marketing:
- Sales:
- Onboarding:
- Service:

Citation:
- [Short quote or paraphrase with anchor]
```

## Do Not

- Do not mistake a feature list, founder resume, slogan, or mission statement for a story.
- Do not position the brand for everyone.
- Do not recommend price cuts as differentiation.
- Do not create messaging that the customer experience cannot prove.
