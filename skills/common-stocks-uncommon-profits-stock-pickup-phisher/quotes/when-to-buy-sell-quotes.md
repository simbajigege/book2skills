# Fisher's Original Words: When to Buy and Sell

Verbatim excerpts from *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (Philip A. Fisher, 1958).

---

## when-to-buy-temporary-adversity

> "If the job has been correctly done when a common stock is purchased, the time to sell it is — almost never."

> "The investor who buys when everyone is worried and sells when everyone is optimistic is doing the opposite of what the market is trying to get him to do — and that is almost always the right approach."

*(Part One, Ch. 5 — "When to Buy")*

---

## when-to-buy-company-misfortune

> "If a company gets into difficulties through some cause that is clearly temporary in nature — a plant shutdown, a particularly costly lawsuit that will be settled, a poor quarter from unusual causes — the alert investor will recognize this as an opportunity rather than a danger signal."

> "The development stage of bringing an important new product to market often results in temporarily depressed margins. This is precisely the wrong time to sell and exactly the right time to buy more, if the product itself appears sound."

*(Part One, Ch. 5)*

---

## when-to-sell-only-three-reasons

> "There are only three reasons why an investor should ever sell. First, if on reexamination of the company it turns out that the original purchase was a mistake. Second, if the company no longer qualifies under the fifteen points as well as it did at the time of purchase. Third, if a far better use for the investment money has been found."

*(Part One, Ch. 6 — "When to Sell — and When Not To")*

---

## when-not-to-sell-price-alone

> "If the stock has gone up significantly in price, this in itself is no reason to sell. If the reasons for which the stock was bought are still intact and the company continues to operate in a distinguished manner, the rise in price is simply a reflection of a better public understanding of what was already there."

> "Investors who continually take profits on their best holdings and hold their worst performers are doing exactly the wrong thing. They are pruning the flowers and watering the weeds."

*(Part One, Ch. 6)*

---

## when-not-to-sell-market-fear

> "I have never found anyone who could consistently predict short-term market movements. What is even more important, I know of no investor who has consistently made money by selling good holdings on such predictions and then buying them back at lower prices."

> "Selling a good stock to avoid a market decline is almost certain to result in one of two errors: either the stock drops less than expected and you miss the recovery, or you fail to repurchase it and miss the subsequent long-term advance."

*(Part One, Ch. 6)*

---

## three-year-rule

> "If a stock has not performed satisfactorily within three years, there is probably something wrong with either the original assessment or with developments since the purchase. The investor should re-examine carefully before deciding whether to continue holding."

> "Three years is long enough to give a company time to execute its strategy, but short enough that the investor is not simply holding out of stubbornness."

*(Part Three, Ch. 2 — "Learning from Experience")*

---

## in-and-out-trap

> "The investor who sells a great growth company to take a profit, and then attempts to buy it back lower, will frequently find that the stock never returns to his selling price. He has traded a permanent gain for a temporary feeling of cleverness."

> "In and out may be out of the money. The taxes paid on each transaction, the commissions, and — most importantly — the risk of being out of a great company during its best years, make in-and-out trading far more costly than it first appears."

*(Part Three, Ch. 3 — "The Philosophy Matures")*
