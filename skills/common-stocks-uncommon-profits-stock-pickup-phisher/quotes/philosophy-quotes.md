# Fisher's Original Words: Investment Philosophy

Verbatim excerpts from *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* (Philip A. Fisher, 1958)
and *Developing an Investment Philosophy* (Part Three).

---

## concentration-not-diversification

> "Diversification for its own sake is not a virtue. The portfolio that contains twenty stocks each of which has been superficially analyzed is far riskier than a portfolio of twelve stocks that have been thoroughly investigated."

> "I believe strongly that the investor who holds stocks in more than twenty companies is probably buying some he knows little about and is therefore taking great risks he is not aware of."

*(Part One, Ch. 9 — "Five More Don'ts for Investors")*

---

## compounding-hold-forever

> "If the growth of a company has been dramatic enough over the years, the investor may find that his paper profits are so large that the tax cost of selling is a significant barrier. But this is not a problem — it is a reward. It means the investment was correct."

> "I know of no formula by which great stocks can be identified at exactly the right time to buy them. But I do know that the investor who buys them at approximately the right time and holds them for many years will do far better than one who tries to trade in and out."

*(Part One, Ch. 6 — "When to Sell — and When Not To")*

---

## market-not-efficient

> "The financial community does not immediately discount all available information in stock prices. It takes time — often years — for the investment community to recognize and correctly appraise the long-term significance of superior management."

> "The fact that a company's true long-term prospects are not reflected in its current price is not an anomaly to be explained away. It is the opportunity that diligent investors exist to exploit."

*(Part Three, Ch. 4 — "Is the Market Efficient?")*

---

## appraisal-drives-price

> "Every significant price move of any individual common stock in relation to stocks as a whole occurs because of a changed appraisal of that stock by the financial community."

> "The appraisal of the financial community can be wrong for years. A company can be outstanding in every fundamental respect and yet be ignored or misunderstood by analysts for a surprisingly long time. This patience of the patient investor is ultimately rewarded."

*(Part Two, Ch. 4 — "The Fourth Dimension: Price of a Conservative Investment")*

---

## low-pe-trap

> "The stock with the low price-earnings ratio is not necessarily cheap. What matters is the ratio of the current price to earnings five to ten years hence — not to earnings of the past twelve months."

> "The investor who buys because a stock looks cheap relative to historical earnings will often find he has bought a company whose best years are behind it. The investor who buys a company whose best years are ahead will seldom find the price-earnings ratio to be low."

*(Part Three, Ch. 1 — "Origins of a Philosophy")*

---

## vivid-spirit-leadership

> "The most valuable intangible in evaluating a company is the 'vivid spirit' of its leaders — that rare quality which inspires an organization to produce results far beyond what any organizational chart can predict."

> "The difference between a good company and a great one very often comes down to one factor: whether the people at the top have this quality of driving their organizations to continuously higher levels of performance."

*(Part Two, Ch. 2 — "The Second Dimension: People")*

---

## honey-jar-margins

> "High profit margins attract competition. Therefore the truly outstanding company must not merely have high margins — it must be so efficient in everything it does that potential competitors see no realistic opportunity to enter and succeed."

> "The company that earns above-average margins because it is genuinely better at what it does — not merely because it has a temporary pricing advantage — is the company that will maintain those margins for years."

*(Part Two, Ch. 3 — "The Third Dimension: Investment Characteristics")*

---

## scuttlebutt-vs-annual-reports

> "The annual report is written to please the stockholder, not to inform the analyst. The investor who relies primarily on annual reports for his information is at a severe disadvantage compared to one who goes out and talks to the people who know the company firsthand."

> "Fifteen minutes of conversation with a knowledgeable competitor or customer will often reveal more about a company's true competitive position than twenty pages of its annual report."

*(Part One, Ch. 2 — "What 'Scuttlebutt' Can Do")*

---

## mistakes-take-small-losses

> "The greatest investment mistakes I have observed come not from investors who are wrong, but from investors who are right in their analysis and wrong in their response to being wrong. The ability to recognize a mistake quickly and accept a small loss is one of the most valuable qualities an investor can have."

> "One of the most important things I learned in my early years of investing is that a small loss accepted promptly is far better than a large loss accepted later. The investor who refuses to accept that he made a mistake is almost certain to make it worse."

*(Part Three, Ch. 4 — "Is the Market Efficient?" — 8-point philosophy)*

---

## patience-under-adversity

> "Most of the major gains I have seen in truly outstanding companies came after a period of adversity — either a specific problem the company was working through, or a general market decline that affected all stocks indiscriminately. The investor who sold during these periods of adversity invariably regretted it."

> "Temporary adversity and permanent deterioration look identical in the short run. The difference emerges only with time. The investor who can distinguish between the two — and act accordingly — has a significant advantage."

*(Part Three, Ch. 3 — "The Philosophy Matures")*
