# Business Adventures — Market Dynamics Quotes

Verbatim excerpts from *Business Adventures: Twelve Classic Tales from the World of Wall Street* (John Brooks, 1969).

---

## market-will-fluctuate

> "The course of the exchange is necessarily mysterious. Those who have written about it have either not understood it, or if they understood, they have not known how to explain it."

> "The things that cause the movement are infinite. Do not expect to know them, but understand that the best indication of movement is movement itself."

*(Joseph de la Vega, "Confusion de Confusiones," 1688 — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## morgans-maxim

> "It will fluctuate."

*(J. P. Morgan's reply when asked what the market would do — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## antiperistasis-defined

> "The market still suffers at times from what de la Vega clinically called 'antiperistasis'—the tendency to reverse itself, then reverse the reversal, and so on."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## expectation-vs-event

> "The expectation of an event creates a much deeper impression … than the event itself."

*(de la Vega — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## tape-delay-consequences

> "The tape delay meant that for the most part the ticker was reporting the prices of an hour before, which in many cases were anywhere from one to ten dollars a share higher than the current prices. It was almost impossible for a broker accepting a selling order to tell his customer what price he might expect to get."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## panic-psychology

> "The bears [that is, the sellers] are completely ruled by fear, trepidation, and nervousness. Rabbits become elephants, brawls in a tavern become rebellions, faint shadows appear to them as signs of chaos."

*(de la Vega, describing a market panic — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## crisis-creates-chaos

> "Whatever else may be said about Tuesday, May 29th, it will be long remembered as the day when there was something very close to a complete breakdown of the reticulated, automated, mind-boggling complex of technical facilities that made nationwide stock-trading possible in a huge country where nearly one out of six adults was a stockholder."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## psychological-gestures

> "The moral may be that psychological gestures on the Exchange are most effective when they are neither intended nor really needed."

*(On the difference between Richard Whitney's staged intervention in 1929 and John Cranley's natural bargain-hunting in 1962 — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## bellwether-stocks

> "Always something of a bellwether, Telephone was now being watched more closely than ever, and each loss of a fraction of a point in its price was the signal for further declines all across the board."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## round-number-psychology

> "The hard fact of Telephone's recovery of almost two points worked together with a purely fortuitous circumstance—the psychological impact of the good, round number 100—to tip the scales."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## specialist-wisdom

> "Telephone is kind of like the sea. Generally, it is calm and kindly. Then all of a sudden a great wind comes and whips up a giant wave. The wave sweeps over and deluges everybody; then it sucks back again. You have to give with it. You can't fight it, any more than King Canute could."

*(George M. L. La Branche, Jr., floor specialist in AT&T — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## crisis-unfathomable

> "In the last analysis, the cause of the 1962 crisis remains unfathomable; what is known is that it occurred, and that something like it could occur again."

*(Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## speculative-cycles

> "I think that people may be more careful for a year or two, and then we may see another speculative buildup followed by another crash, and so on until God makes people less greedy."

*(Anonymous Wall Street veteran — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*

---

## sweetness-of-honey

> "It is foolish to think that you can withdraw from the Exchange after you have tasted the sweetness of the honey."

*(de la Vega — Chapter 1: The Fluctuation)*
