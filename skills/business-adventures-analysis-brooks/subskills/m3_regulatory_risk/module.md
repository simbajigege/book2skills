# Skill: Regulatory Risk Review (Business Adventures — Module 3)

**Knowledge source:** *Business Adventures* by John Brooks  
**Reference library:** `references/case_library.md`  
**Standalone use:** This module can run independently or as part of `../../SKILL.md`.

## When to Use

Use this module for insider information, disclosure timing, antitrust, price fixing, tax, tolerated industry practices, enforcement lag, or "everyone does it" defenses.

## Module Inputs

- Practice or decision being reviewed
- Who knew what, and when
- What was publicly disclosed and whether it was usable
- Stakeholders affected by the information or practice
- Current enforcement, public pressure, and rule-maker context

## Module Outputs

- Regulatory risk level: low, medium, high, or uncertain
- Materiality and usable-disclosure assessment
- Latent rule-change risk
- Recommended disclosure, control, or stop action

## Execution Flow

### Overview

```text
Regulatory risk
├── Step 1: Define the practice
├── Step 2: Apply materiality and timing tests
├── Step 3: Test public usability of disclosure
├── Step 4: Separate custom from legality
└── Step 5: Assess rule-change pressure
```

### Step 1: Define the Practice

**Core question:** What exactly is being tolerated, exploited, hidden, or selectively disclosed?

**Decision rules:**

- If insiders can act before outsiders receive usable information, risk rises.
- If the defense is mainly "industry custom," risk remains latent.
- If the practice harms customers, investors, competitors, or market integrity, visibility can turn tolerance into enforcement.

**Ask the user:** Who benefits from the information gap or rule ambiguity?

**Citation instruction:** Cite `REF-M3-001` or `REF-M3-002` for insider-information advantage.

### Step 2: Apply Materiality and Timing Tests

**Core question:** Would a reasonable investor, customer, competitor, or regulator care before making a decision?

**Decision rules:**

| Test | High-risk signal |
|---|---|
| Materiality | The fact would change price, consent, buying, or enforcement behavior |
| Timing | Favored parties can act before outsiders |
| Accessibility | Disclosure exists technically but not practically |
| Enforcement pressure | Public loss or scandal could force action |

**Citation instruction:** Cite `REF-M3-003` for stacked-deck or unfair-market framing.

### Step 3: Assess Rule-Change Pressure

**Core question:** Could a visible crisis force rule-makers to define a new boundary?

**Decision rules:**

- Dormant enforcement is not permission.
- A tolerated practice becomes fragile when losses become public.
- Antitrust and tax boundaries can shift when behavior appears coordinated or abusive.

**Citation instruction:** Cite `REF-M3-004`, `REF-M3-005`, or `REF-M3-006` depending on the risk type.

## Do Not

- Do not equate common practice with low risk.
- Do not treat technical disclosure as adequate if outsiders cannot use it.
- Do not give legal advice; frame the output as Brooks-style risk diagnosis.
