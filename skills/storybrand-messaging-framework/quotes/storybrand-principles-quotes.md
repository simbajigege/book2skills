# Building a StoryBrand — StoryBrand Principles Quotes

Verbatim excerpts from *Building a StoryBrand: Clarify Your Message So Customers Will Listen* (Donald Miller, 2025).

---

## story-organizes-information

> "Story helps because it is a sense-making mechanism. Essentially, story formulas put everything in order so the brain doesn't have to work to understand what's on."

*(Chapter 1: The Key to Being Seen, Heard, and Understood)*

---

## brain-survive-and-thrive

> "The overriding function of the brain is to help an individual survive and thrive."

*(Chapter 1: The Key to Being Seen, Heard, and Understood)*

---

## story-makes-music

> "Story is similar to music. A good story takes a series of random events and truths and distills them into the essence of what really matters."

*(Chapter 2: The Secret Weapon That Will Grow Your Business)*

---

## think-different-lesson

> "When Apple began filtering their communication to make it simple and relevant, they actually stopped featuring computers in most of their advertising."

*(Chapter 2: The Secret Weapon That Will Grow Your Business)*

---

## hero-in-a-hole

> "Every one of our customers is a 'hero in a hole' looking for a way out."

*(Chapter 3: The Simple SB7 Framework)*

---

## three-levels-problems

> "There are three levels of problems a customer encounters. In stories, heroes encounter external, internal, and philosophical problems."

*(Chapter 3 & 5: The Simple SB7 Framework / Has a Problem)*

---

## guide-positioning

> "Positioning yourself as Yoda and your audience as Luke Skywalker. This small but powerful shift honors the journey of the audience and positions us as a leader providing wisdom."

*(Chapter 3: The Simple SB7 Framework)*

---

## tidal-fatal-mistake

> "The fatal mistake many brands make is positioning themselves as the hero in the story rather than the guide."

*(Chapter 6: And Meets a Guide)*

---

## story-gap-power

> "Attention rises and lowers with the opening and closing of a story gap."

*(Chapter 4: A Character)*

---

## conflict-gets-attention

> "Readers want to fret. Conflict gets people's attention."

*(Chapter 5: Has a Problem)*

---

## villain-focus

> "The villain is the number one device storytellers use to give conflict a clear point of focus."

*(Chapter 5: Has a Problem)*

---

## plan-removes-confusion

> "What customers are looking for is a clear path we've laid out that takes away any confusion they might have about how the process of getting out of the hole is going to work."

*(Chapter 7: Who Gives Them a Plan)*

---

## two-types-cta

> "There are two kinds of calls to action: direct (buy now) and transitional (small commitments)."

*(Chapter 8: And Calls Them to Action)*

---

## whats-at-stake

> "Brands that don't warn customers about potential failure fail to answer 'What's at stake?'"

*(Chapter 9: That Helps Them Avoid Failure)*

---

## aspirational-identity

> "The aspirational identity—who does your customer want to become?"

*(Chapter 11: People Want Your Brand to Participate in Their Transformation)*

---

## one-liner-formula

> "One-liner formula: Problem + Solution + Result"

*(Chapter 12: Define Your Controlling Idea)*

---

## website-hierarchy

> "Website hierarchy: 1) Header with clear offer, 2) Stakes, 3) Value proposition, 4) Guide section, 5) Plan, 6) CTA, 7) Junk drawer"

*(Chapter 14: How to Get Your Website Right)*
