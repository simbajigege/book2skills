# The Little Book of Common Sense Investing — Index Fund Quotes

Verbatim excerpts from *The Little Book of Common Sense Investing* (John C. Bogle, 2017, 10th Anniversary Edition).

---

## own-entire-market

> "Own all of the nation's publicly held businesses at very low cost via an index fund, and hold it forever."

*(Introduction)*

---

## common-sense-investing

> "Successful investing is all about common sense. As Warren Buffett has said, it is simple, but it is not easy."

*(Introduction)*

---

## cost-matters-hypothesis

> "It's not the Efficient Market Hypothesis; it's the Cost Matters Hypothesis. Gross return in the market, minus the costs of intermediation, equals the net return actually earned by investors."

*(Chapter 5: The Cost Matters Hypothesis)*

---

## alpha-negative-sum

> "Alpha is a zero-sum game before costs and a negative-sum game after costs."

*(Chapter 4: The Quest for Alpha)*

---

## relentless-arithmetic

> "The relentless rules of humble arithmetic — the gross return on the stock market, minus the costs of investing, equals the net return to investors as a group."

*(Chapter 2: The Relentless Rules of Humble Arithmetic)*

---

## we-get-what-we-dont-pay-for

> "As a group, we investors don't get what we pay for. We get precisely what we don't pay for. The less we pay, the more we get."

*(Chapter 5: The Cost Matters Hypothesis)*

---

## index-fund-eliminates-risks

> "The traditional index fund eliminates the risk of picking individual stocks, the risk of emphasizing certain market sectors, and the risk of manager selection."

*(Chapter 3: The Index Fund)*

---

## reversion-to-mean

> "Reversion to the mean is the most powerful force in investing. Top-performing funds tend to revert to average or below."

*(Chapter 11: Reversion to the Mean)*

---

## simplicity-majesty

> "Simple, low-cost investing beats complex, high-cost investing. The majesty of simplicity and parsimony."

*(Chapter 13: Simplicity and Parsimony)*

---

## investments-perform-better-than-investors

> "Investments usually perform better than investors."

*(Chapter 7: The Grand Illusion)*
